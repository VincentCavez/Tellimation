# SKILL Micro-Objectives: Sentence-Level Structure
# Grammar & vocabulary production, complexity & accuracy in oral narration
# Target: children ages 7-11 narrating pixel-art scenes
#
# Each micro-objective maps to one or more error types tracked by the system.
# The NEG generator uses these to create fine-grained assessment targets.

# ============================================================================
# A. NOUN PHRASES — naming and describing entities
# ============================================================================

A1. Entity naming (IDENTITY)
    The child names each entity using a recognisable noun.
    - Acceptable: common nouns ("a rabbit"), proper nouns if story gives one,
      near-synonyms ("bunny" for rabbit).
    - Error: vague pronoun with no antecedent ("it", "that thing"), wrong
      category noun ("the dog" for a rabbit).

A2. Determiner usage (IDENTITY, QUANTITY)
    The child uses an appropriate article or determiner before nouns.
    - Indefinite for first mention ("a rabbit"), definite for subsequent
      mention or shared knowledge ("the rabbit").
    - Demonstratives when pointing ("this rock", "that tree").
    - Quantifiers when multiple ("three birds", "some flowers").

A3. Colour adjectives (PROPERTY_COLOR)
    The child includes colour when it is visually distinctive.
    - Basic: red, blue, green, brown, orange, yellow, purple, white, black, grey.
    - Specific: golden, dark brown, bright orange, pale blue.
    - Compound: "red and white", "dark green with yellow spots".

A4. Size adjectives (PROPERTY_SIZE)
    The child uses size descriptors when relevant to the scene.
    - Absolute: tiny, small, little, big, large, huge, enormous, giant.
    - Relative: bigger than, smaller than, the biggest.

A5. Texture / material adjectives (PROPERTY_STATE)
    The child describes surface or material qualities.
    - Examples: fluffy, rough, smooth, shiny, wooden, rocky, soft, hard,
      furry, scaly, feathery.

A6. State / condition adjectives (PROPERTY_STATE)
    The child describes the entity's state when narratively relevant.
    - Physical: sleeping, awake, broken, whole, open, closed, wet, dry.
    - Emotional: happy, sad, angry, scared, surprised, calm, curious.

A7. Multi-modifier noun phrases (PROPERTY_COLOR, PROPERTY_SIZE, PROPERTY_STATE)
    The child stacks two or more modifiers in a noun phrase.
    - Example: "a big fluffy orange cat" (size + texture + colour + noun).
    - Age 7-8 target: at least one modifier before noun.
    - Age 9-11 target: two or more modifiers in natural order
      (size > quality > colour > noun).

# ============================================================================
# B. VERB PHRASES — actions, manner, and tense
# ============================================================================

B1. Action verb specificity (ACTION)
    The child uses a precise verb rather than a generic one.
    - Generic (avoid): go, do, move, be.
    - Specific (preferred): hop, creep, soar, splash, climb, slide, hide,
      peek, chase, float, sink, bounce.

B2. Manner adverbs (MANNER)
    The child qualifies the action with an adverb or adverbial phrase.
    - Speed: quickly, slowly, fast.
    - Care: carefully, gently, recklessly.
    - Sound: quietly, loudly.
    - Emotion: happily, angrily, nervously.

B3. Verb + manner pairing (ACTION, MANNER)
    The child combines a specific verb with a manner descriptor.
    - Example: "the rabbit hops quickly" not just "the rabbit hops".
    - Example: "the owl flies silently through the night".

B4. Tense consistency — within sentence (TEMPORAL)
    The child uses a single tense within one sentence.
    - Error: "the rabbit hops and then he was jumping" (present + past).

B5. Tense consistency — across narration (TEMPORAL)
    The child maintains the chosen tense across multiple sentences.
    - Narration default: simple present ("the cat sits on the rock")
      or simple past ("the cat sat on the rock").
    - Error: switching between present and past without temporal marker.

B6. Progressive aspect (TEMPORAL, ACTION)
    The child uses -ing forms to describe ongoing actions when appropriate.
    - "The bird is flying over the tree" (ongoing scene action).
    - Distinguishes habitual ("the bird flies") from in-progress
      ("the bird is flying").

# ============================================================================
# C. SPATIAL EXPRESSIONS — location and position
# ============================================================================

C1. Basic prepositions (SPATIAL)
    The child uses accurate prepositions to describe position.
    - on, in, under, behind, in front of, next to, beside, between,
      above, below, near, far from.

C2. Spatial relation accuracy (SPATIAL, RELATIONAL)
    The described position matches the scene.
    - Error: saying "under the tree" when the entity is on a branch
      (should be "on" or "in").

C3. Relative position chains (SPATIAL, RELATIONAL)
    The child locates entities relative to each other, not just in isolation.
    - "The frog is on the rock next to the pond."
    - "The owl is in the tree behind the house."

C4. Directional expressions (SPATIAL, ACTION)
    The child uses direction when describing movement.
    - "The bird flies over the mountain" (not just "the bird flies").
    - Directional prepositions: into, out of, through, across, along,
      towards, away from, up, down, around.

# ============================================================================
# D. TEMPORAL EXPRESSIONS — sequence and time
# ============================================================================

D1. Temporal connectors (TEMPORAL)
    The child uses words to sequence events within the narration.
    - Sequence: first, then, next, after that, finally, at last.
    - Simultaneous: while, as, at the same time, meanwhile.
    - Contrast: but then, suddenly, all of a sudden.

D2. Before / after reasoning (TEMPORAL)
    The child correctly orders events that have a before-after relationship.
    - "Before the rabbit jumped, it looked around."
    - "After the rain stopped, the flowers opened."

D3. Tense markers for time reference (TEMPORAL)
    The child marks time shifts with explicit vocabulary.
    - Past: "earlier", "before", "yesterday", "once upon a time".
    - Present: "now", "right now", "at this moment".
    - Future/intention: "soon", "about to", "going to".

# ============================================================================
# E. SENTENCE COMPLEXITY — structure and embedding
# ============================================================================

E1. Complete simple sentences
    The child produces grammatically complete sentences with
    subject + verb (+ object/complement).
    - "The rabbit sits on the rock." (not "rabbit... rock...").
    - Fragments are expected at age 7-8 but should decrease over time.

E2. Compound sentences — coordinating conjunctions
    The child joins two clauses with and, but, so, or.
    - "The cat is orange and it is sitting on the fence."
    - "The rabbit tried to jump but it was too far."

E3. Complex sentences — subordinating conjunctions
    The child uses because, when, while, if, although, since, after, before.
    - "The frog is hiding because it is scared."
    - "While the bird sings, the cat watches."
    - Age 7-8: at least one per narration.
    - Age 9-11: regular use to explain causality and context.

E4. Relative clauses (who, which, that)
    The child embeds a relative clause to add information about an entity.
    - "The rabbit, who has long ears, is sitting on the rock."
    - "The tree that is next to the pond has green leaves."

E5. Varied sentence openers
    The child does not begin every sentence the same way.
    - Vary with: adverbials ("Suddenly, ..."), prepositional phrases
      ("On the rock, ..."), temporal markers ("Then, ..."),
      subjects ("The owl ...", "A small frog ...").

# ============================================================================
# F. QUANTITY & PLURALISATION
# ============================================================================

F1. Singular vs. plural agreement (QUANTITY)
    The child uses correct number marking.
    - "Three birds are flying" not "three birds is flying".
    - "One fish swims" not "one fish swim".

F2. Quantifier accuracy (QUANTITY)
    The child uses the correct quantity or appropriate quantifier.
    - Exact: "two cats", "three flowers".
    - Approximate: "some birds", "a few rocks", "many stars".
    - Error: "a bird" when there are clearly three.

# ============================================================================
# G. COHERENCE & COMPLETENESS
# ============================================================================

G1. Entity coverage (OMISSION)
    The child mentions all salient entities in the scene, not just one.
    - At minimum: main characters + key objects.
    - Full coverage: background elements that add to the setting.

G2. Avoiding redundancy (REDUNDANCY)
    The child does not repeat the same information unnecessarily.
    - Error: "the big cat is big and it is big and orange".
    - Acceptable: repeating an entity name for clarity across sentences.

G3. Non-existent entity claims (EXISTENCE)
    The child does not invent entities not present in the scene.
    - Error: "there is a dragon in the sky" when no dragon exists.

G4. Relation accuracy (RELATIONAL)
    The child correctly describes relationships between entities.
    - Possession: "the rabbit's carrot", "the bird in its nest".
    - Interaction: "the cat is watching the fish", not
      "the cat is friends with the fish" (when no such relation exists).
